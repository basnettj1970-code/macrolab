GDP — Gross Domestic Product

The total value of everything a country produces in a year. When GDP grows, the economy is expanding — companies earn more, workers are hired, and living standards tend to rise. Negative GDP growth for two quarters in a row is officially called a recession.

Macro concept
💸

Inflation

How fast prices rise over time. A small amount (around 2%) is healthy — it means the economy is active. Too much inflation erodes your purchasing power. Too little can signal a stagnant economy. Central banks like the Bank of England target 2%.

👷

Unemployment

The share of people who want a job but can't find one. Around 4–5% is considered "full employment" — some turnover is natural as people change careers. High unemployment means wasted human potential and lower consumer spending across the economy.

Labour market
⚖️

The Phillips curve trade-off

Low unemployment tends to push inflation up — when workers are scarce, wages rise, people spend more, and prices follow. This means policymakers face a constant balancing act: stimulate too hard and inflation spikes; tighten too hard and jobs disappear.

Key trade-off
🏦

Monetary policy

The central bank's toolkit — mainly interest rates and money supply. Raise rates to slow a hot economy and fight inflation. Cut rates to stimulate growth during a downturn. In the UK this is set by the Bank of England's Monetary Policy Committee.

Policy tool
🏛️

Fiscal policy

Government decisions on spending and taxes. Spending more (or cutting taxes) injects money into the economy — a "stimulus." Spending less (or raising taxes) removes money — "austerity." Unlike monetary policy, fiscal policy is set by elected governments, not central banks.
